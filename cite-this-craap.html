CRAAP Policy

By Alex • Published: 6 December 2025 • Updated: 6 December 2025

Trust in information online is becoming increasingly difficult. Misreporting, selective editing, agenda-driven commentary and AI-generated misinformation have made it harder than ever to judge whether something is credible.

Even long-established institutions have recently made serious errors — such as the BBC facing criticism for editing part of a political speech in a way that changed its meaning. Incidents like this highlight a broader truth: no single source is automatically authoritative anymore. This CRAAP Policy exists to explain how information on CyberAlex is produced, what its limitations are, and how you should evaluate it.

At a Glance

  • No institution is infallible — including this one.
  • This page explains how to assess content on CyberAlex.
  • Citations and versioning aim to support transparency.
  • You are encouraged to verify information independently.

Summary: This policy helps you evaluate credibility in a digital landscape where trust must be earned, not assumed.

Why a CRAAP Policy?

The internet is saturated with conflicting claims, political spin, AI-generated articles, and content engineered for engagement rather than truth. Studies from Ofcom, the EU Digital Services Act transparency reports, and multiple academic papers all highlight the same trend: misinformation is now widespread, fast-moving, and increasingly difficult to detect.

In this landscape, credibility cannot simply be inherited from reputation. Even respected news organisations, universities, government agencies and research bodies occasionally publish material that is incomplete, misleading, or later corrected. This does not make them “bad” sources — it simply means that critical evaluation is essential everywhere.

The CRAAP framework (Credibility, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose) is a tool to help you assess whether information is trustworthy. This page adapts that framework to explain how CyberAlex content should be interpreted.

Credibility

Content on CyberAlex is written by me (Alex), based on academic qualifications, practical cybersecurity experience, and ongoing research. Where relevant, I cite external sources or include links so you can verify material independently.

However — and this is important — you should never assume credibility solely because it is published here. Cross-check information where necessary, especially for academic or professional use.

Relevance

Most content on CyberAlex relates to cybersecurity, risk, governance, motoring analysis, photography, or personal technical projects. Page titles are designed to accurately reflect content so you can quickly judge whether it is relevant to your needs.

Authority

My authority comes from formal education (BSc Computer Security, MSc Cyber Security, Risk & Resilience), practical security work, and the academic writing I have produced.

Still, I do not claim to be an ultimate authority. Cybersecurity evolves rapidly, and new evidence or standards may supersede earlier assumptions. Treat CyberAlex as a knowledgeable contributor — one source among many.

Accuracy

The accuracy of content varies by category:

  • Formal documents follow referencing standards and aim for high accuracy.
  • Technical tools are transparent, client-side, and inspectable.
  • Blog posts may include personal perspective or evolving understanding.
  • Photography and creative work are subjective by nature.

Errors may occur; when identified, corrections will be made and documented in the page’s change log.

Purpose

The intent behind each type of content is different:

  • Reports aim to educate or inform.
  • Tools aim to provide practical, transparent utility.
  • Affiliated sites each have their own stated purpose.
  • Blog posts aim to share experience and insight.

Across all of CyberAlex, the purpose is clarity, honesty and usefulness — not persuasion, monetisation or narrative shaping.
